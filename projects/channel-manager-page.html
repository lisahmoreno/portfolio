Channel Manager Page

A research-led extranet feature that makes connectivity health visible to hotels — so a broken channel manager connection is caught near-real-time, not discovered days later when bookings stop coming in.

User research Discovery B2B · Hospitality Extranet

The problem

A channel manager is the connectivity tool a hotel uses to push its availability and rates out to all sales channels — our portals plus OTAs. For hotels that depend on it, it's not a nice-to-have; it's the mechanism that keeps their inventory live everywhere at once.

When the connection broke, nothing in the extranet told them. Stale availability propagated silently across every portal. The first signal was usually a drop in bookings — which a hotel might not notice for days, and which account managers (KAMs) were expected to catch through their own daily troubleshooting routines. A broken link was a routine part of KAM work, not an exceptional event. The extranet had no surface for this at all.

Discovery: two user groups, two different problems

I ran 13 interviews across two rounds (March–June 2026) — 7 hotel partners and 6 KAMs — before any design work started. The two groups had structurally different versions of the same problem.

KAMs (6 interviews)
  • Issues always arrived reactively — hotels had already noticed missing bookings before contacting support
  • KAMs had built informal workarounds: periodic manual checks, pattern recognition for failure modes, mental lists of "risky" hotels
  • No systematic tool existed — each KAM managed 200–380 hotels, ~80% using a CM connection
  • First-level support frequently needed engineering to diagnose — no diagnostic surface existed
  • Unanimous on cross-hotel visibility: 6/6 identified it as a top need (Tableau sunset made this urgent)
Hotel partners (7 interviews)
  • Core asymmetry: the CM shows green on the hotel's side while nothing is arriving at Getaway — hotels had no way to know
  • Errors when they appeared were opaque: "transfer error" with no specificity on room type, date, or cause
  • Mapping failures could go unnoticed for days — one hotel ran 6–7 days without live inventory before catching it themselves
  • Reliance varied significantly — from family-run properties that "trust the system" to technical chain managers who wanted raw XML access

Four hotel archetypes

The hotel partner sample wasn't uniform — we identified four distinct archetypes with meaningfully different needs. This shaped the progressive disclosure approach in design: a single UI couldn't serve all four.

HP-Light
Family-run, owner-operated
"We trust the system." Low diagnostic need. Wants simple status, plain language — not technical detail.
HP-Mid
Online / reservations roles
Bookings not arriving at PMS; backup prices going live. Needs clear alerts and actionable next steps. Prototype was "self-explanatory" on first view.
HP-Power
Technical chain managers
Wants depth: filters, re-push controls, XML/raw data access, ability to self-diagnose. Multi-property overview essential.
Handling Agent
External sales agency
Manages ~20 hotels on behalf of owners. Wants restricted edit rights for hotel staff, audit log ("who changed what"), and clear-text errors — not XML.

One finding validated a design risk we'd flagged in v1: we weren't sure whether limiting hotel-side editing permissions would feel paternalistic. By v2, a power-user hotel partner (HP 9) actively confirmed they want those restrictions — hotel staff breaking CM settings was a known pain point, not a hypothetical. The role model went from "possible paternalism" to "HP-requested."

The design concept

A dedicated Channel Manager page in the hotel extranet with two things: current connection status, made visible; and proactive alerts when the connection breaks. The shift from reactive to proactive is the core product decision — instead of waiting for a hotel to notice bookings have slowed, the system flags the issue at detection time.

Research confirmed progressive disclosure as the right structural approach: a clean default view for HP-Light and HP-Mid, with opt-in depth (technical error detail, XML toggle, per-room-category breakdown) for HP-Power. A single dense view would have worked for nobody.

The page needed to answer different questions for three audiences:

Audience Question the page answers
Hotel partner Is my connection working right now? If not, what specifically broke — which room type, which date — and what do I do?
KAM Which of my hotels have connection issues right now? Can I resolve them without engineering?
First-level support Is this a CM issue? Can I diagnose and close the ticket without escalating?

Error messaging was a specific design target: research found that "transfer error" — the existing message — was meaningless to anyone trying to act on it. The design specifies errors at arrangement + room category + date level, with plain-language descriptions and sender/receiver attribution. XML detail is available as a toggle for power users, not the default.

Status and intended impact

User research is complete; the page is in design and build, targeted for delivery in July 2026.

Pre-launch targets: cut issue detection from days to near-real-time; reduce channel-manager-related support load by an estimated ~30%; enable first-level support to resolve connection issues without engineering involvement. These are targets, not measured outcomes — the case study will be updated once the feature is live and data is in.
