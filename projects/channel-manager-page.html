Channel Manager Page

A research-led extranet feature that makes connectivity health visible to hotels — so a broken channel manager connection is caught near-real-time, not discovered days later when bookings stop coming in.

User research Discovery B2B · Hospitality Extranet

The problem

A channel manager is the connectivity tool a hotel uses to push its availability and rates out to all sales channels — our portals plus OTAs. For hotels that depend on it, it's not a nice-to-have; it's the mechanism that keeps their inventory live everywhere at once.

When the connection broke, nothing in the extranet told them. Stale availability would propagate silently across every portal. The first signal that something was wrong was usually a drop in bookings — which a hotel might not notice for days, and which account managers (KAMs) were expected to catch reactively through their own daily troubleshooting routines. A broken link was a routine part of KAM work, not an exceptional event.

The extranet had no surface for this at all. Hotels using a channel manager had no way to see the connection status, no way to know if something was wrong, and no way to act without calling support.

Discovery first

Before shaping any solution, I ran a discovery phase with two distinct user groups — because what the problem looks like from the KAM side and what it looks like from the hotel side are quite different.

KAMs were the ones absorbing the cost. Six interviews surfaced a consistent pattern: connection issues came in reactively, usually after a hotel had already noticed missing bookings. KAMs had built their own informal workarounds — periodic manual checks, recognised failure patterns, heuristics for which hotels were "risky." The problem was well-understood but there was no systematic tool to support it.

Hotel partners had a different experience: they often didn't know what a channel manager connection failure even looked like, and they had no place to go in the extranet to check. Their reliance on the connection varied significantly — some hotels had channel managers configured but rarely used them; others had their entire availability pipeline running through the connection. Segmenting by reliance level shaped how we thought about the feature's priority surface.

The findings were synthesised into a shared research document before any design work started — so the designer and I were working from the same picture rather than building from separate interpretations of the same conversations.

The design concept

A dedicated Channel Manager page in the hotel extranet with two things: current connection status, made visible; and proactive alerts when the connection breaks, so the hotel and KAM know immediately rather than after bookings dry up.

The page needed to work for two audiences with different levels of technical understanding. A hotel partner needs to know: is my connection working right now, and if not, what do I do? A KAM needs to know: which of my hotels have issues, and can I resolve them without pulling in engineering? First-level support handling a channel manager ticket without needing to escalate was part of the intended design — the page should surface enough diagnostic information to be useful, not just a status light.

The shift from reactive to proactive is the core product decision. Instead of waiting for a hotel to notice bookings have slowed, the system flags the issue at detection time. That's a fundamentally different operating model for both the hotel and the KAM.

Status and intended impact

User research is complete; the page is in design and build, with live status plus logging and alerting targeted for delivery in July 2026.

Pre-launch targets: cut issue detection from days to near-real-time; reduce channel-manager-related support load by an estimated ~30%; enable first-level support to resolve connection issues without engineering involvement. These are targets, not measured outcomes — the case study will be updated once the feature is live and data is in.
