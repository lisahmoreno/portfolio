Children's Pricing — UX Exploration

Turning a per-offer chore into a one-time setup — and the data analysis, product design, and change communication work that made it possible.

UX Design Prototyping Data analysis B2B · Hospitality
Live mockups ↗ GitHub ↗

The problem

In our hotel extranet, children's prices lived at the offer level. A hotel with many arrangements had to enter the same age-tier prices over and over — once per offer, every time. In practice this meant a lot of inconsistent or simply missing kids pricing across a hotel's offers, and a lot of frustration from hoteliers who couldn't see why they were being asked to repeat themselves.

On top of that, the pricing model used the concept of an "Anteil" — a proportional share of the full-payer price — which confused non-expert hoteliers who just wanted to say "kids pay €15 a night" or "30% off the adult rate." The terminology was technically correct but practically opaque.

The product decision: finding the right level

The core question was where in the data model children's pricing should live. The existing answer — at offer level — was causing all the pain. Most hotels set the same children's prices across all their offers; the exceptions were the minority, not the rule.

The concept I designed: hotel-level defaults with per-age-tier overrides at offer level. A hotel sets its children's pricing once on a dedicated hotel page. Those prices are inherited automatically by every arrangement with "book with kids" enabled. Where a specific offer genuinely needs a different price for one age tier, it can override just that tier — the others continue to inherit. One price type per hotel (fixed € or % discount), applied consistently unless explicitly overridden.

This inheritance-with-override model is a common pattern in B2B configuration UIs, but the design work is in the details: what "empty" means in a field that might be inheriting, how to show the user whether a price is theirs or a default, and how to handle the case where someone overrides a price but forgets to set a matching price type.

The UX details that mattered most: an auto-calculated "full payer" row so hoteliers could sanity-check relative prices at a glance; "Kostenlos" (free) badges for zero-priced age tiers instead of a blank €0 field; a progress indicator so hotels could see how complete their setup was; and empty states with a direct CTA rather than just an empty table. The "Anteil" label was replaced with "Ermäßigung" (discount) with a one-time explainer — a small change that removed a consistent point of confusion.

What made it complicated

The feature sat on top of a live migration between two backend systems with incompatible data models. The legacy system (KU) stored children's prices by explicit age ranges (e.g. 0–5), distinguished between 1st, 2nd, and 3rd child, and tracked different prices by number of adults present. The new system (KMW) used only an upper age bound, made no distinction by child number, and had no adult-count differentiation. None of these mapped cleanly.

The data analysis before design was as important as the design itself. How should a "1st child 0–5: €50, 2nd child: €30" price be migrated to a system that doesn't know which child it is? Which backend should resolve the inheritance fallback — the API (recommended, so the portal notices no difference) or the portal itself (error-prone)? What does "empty = inherits from hotel" look like in a database that previously stored per-offer prices everywhere?

Getting those questions answered before writing specs meant the mockups could be grounded in what was actually buildable, and engineering reviews didn't surface surprises.

Result

Children's pricing went from a repeated per-arrangement task to a single hotel-level setup. For hotels with many arrangements, that's an estimated ~70% reduction in repetitive data entry — fewer fields to maintain, and fewer inconsistent or missing prices across their offers as a result. The change communication concept (in-app banners + changelog entries) shipped alongside the feature to make the transition legible for existing hotels without a support spike.
