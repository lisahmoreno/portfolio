AI Hotel Attribute Scanner

A prototype that crawls hotel websites and uses Claude AI to automatically detect amenities — exploring whether AI can replace a time-consuming manual content process in hospitality.

The problem

Hotel booking platforms live and die on data quality. When a guest searches for hotels with an indoor pool or a Finnish sauna, the platform needs to know which hotels actually have those things — and that information has to be kept up to date as hotels change their offerings.

The traditional approach is to ask hotels to fill in their own attributes through an extranet form. In practice, this means a lot of incomplete profiles: hotels that don't log in, hotels that miss the new fields, hotels that tick "sauna" without specifying what type. Content teams then spend time manually looking up hotels and filling in gaps.

What I wanted to test

The hypothesis was simple: hotel websites already contain most of the attribute information we need. A hotel that has a Finnish sauna will almost certainly mention it somewhere on their site — usually on a wellness page, in a room description, or in an FAQ. If an AI can read those pages and reliably extract structured data, we could at minimum pre-fill the attribute form for hotels, or flag gaps in existing profiles for review.

I wanted to test two things:

  • Could an AI reliably distinguish between a hotel that explicitly mentions "Finnish sauna" and one that just says "wellness area" (which tells you nothing)?
  • Would the confidence split (high vs. needs review) be calibrated well enough to be useful — i.e., could you trust the high-confidence results without checking them manually?

Approaches I considered

The obvious alternative to AI is rule-based web scraping — search for keywords like "sauna", "pool", "WiFi" across the page text. This is cheap and fast, but it misses synonyms, multilingual content, and context. "Wellness area" won't match "sauna" even when the page later mentions one. You also can't distinguish "indoor pool" from "outdoor pool" or "pool bar" without more complex NLP.

I considered a crowdsourced approach — ask hotels to verify AI-generated suggestions rather than fill in blank forms — but that still requires the hotel to take action. The goal was to reduce the burden on hotels, not just change the interaction pattern.

A supervised ML model trained on labelled hotel descriptions was another option, but that requires a labelled dataset we don't have and significant infrastructure to train and maintain. For a prototype, an LLM is the faster and more flexible path.

How the prototype works

The tool takes a hotel website URL, crawls the main page and up to 8 relevant subpages (wellness, rooms, dining, FAQ, contact, etc.), strips navigation and boilerplate, and sends the extracted text to Claude with a structured list of ~90 known attribute types.

Claude returns each attribute it finds as either high confidence (explicitly mentioned, with a direct quote as evidence) or needs review (implied or ambiguous). Attributes not found at all are left unchecked.

The confidence split matters for the workflow: high-confidence items could reasonably be auto-applied, while the "needs review" pile gets a human look. This keeps the process fast without fully removing oversight.

One design decision I spent time on: the AI had to be told explicitly that a wellness area name (e.g. "Wellness Oasis") is not evidence for specific facilities. Without that instruction, it would confidently report a Finnish sauna just because the hotel had a nice-sounding spa name. Getting the prompt right to avoid this kind of false positive was the main calibration challenge.

What I learned

The AI is genuinely good at the task when the website content is explicit — it picks up specific mentions reliably and the evidence quotes make it easy to verify. It's much weaker when hotels are vague or use marketing language ("state-of-the-art wellness facilities") instead of plain descriptions. Which, unfortunately, is very common.

The confidence calibration worked better than I expected. High-confidence results were mostly correct; the needs-review pile appropriately flagged edge cases. But "accuracy" is hard to define here — the ground truth is what the hotel actually has, not what they say on their website, and those aren't always the same.

What would need to happen to take this further

The prototype proves the concept works at the individual hotel level. Taking it to production would require:

  • Scale testing — does quality hold up across hundreds of hotels in different languages and website styles?
  • Feedback loop — if hotels or content teams correct an AI suggestion, that correction should inform future scans of the same hotel
  • Change detection — the useful version isn't a one-time scan but an ongoing monitor that flags when a hotel's website suggests their attributes have changed
  • Legal/accuracy liability — attributes like accessibility features or allergy-friendly rooms have real consequences if wrong. The tool's confidence system would need tighter calibration for these, or they'd need to stay human-reviewed always
