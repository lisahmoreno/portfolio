AI Hotel Attribute Scanner

A prototype that crawls hotel websites and uses Claude AI to automatically detect amenities — exploring whether AI can replace a time-consuming manual content process in hospitality.

The problem

Hotel booking platforms depend on accurate attribute data — when a guest filters for "Finnish sauna" or "indoor pool", the platform needs to know which hotels actually have those things. The standard approach is asking hotels to fill in an extranet form. In practice: incomplete profiles, missed fields, and content teams spending hours manually checking hotel websites to fill the gaps.

The hypothesis: hotel websites already contain most of this information. If an AI can reliably extract structured attribute data from a crawl, you could at minimum pre-fill the form for hotels — or flag stale profiles for review.

How it works

The tool takes a hotel URL, crawls the main page and up to 8 relevant subpages (wellness, rooms, dining, FAQ, etc.), strips boilerplate, and sends the text to Claude with a structured list of ~90 attribute types.

Claude returns each attribute as either high confidence (explicitly mentioned, with a quote as evidence) or needs review (implied or ambiguous). High-confidence results could be auto-applied; the needs-review pile gets a human look. The confidence split is what makes this workflow-useful rather than just a novelty.

The trickiest prompt engineering: telling the AI that a wellness area name ("Wellness Oasis") is not evidence for specific facilities. Without that instruction, it would confidently flag a Finnish sauna just because the hotel had an evocative spa name. Getting calibration right — especially for false positives — was the main technical challenge.

What I learned

The AI works well when hotel websites are specific. It's much weaker against marketing language — "state-of-the-art wellness facilities" tells you nothing. Which is unfortunately common. The confidence split helped: high-confidence results held up, and the needs-review pile correctly flagged the ambiguous cases.

One thing "accuracy" doesn't capture: the ground truth is what the hotel has, not what they say on their site. Those don't always match. That gap matters more for high-stakes attributes (accessibility, allergy-friendly) and would need tighter calibration — or a permanent human-review requirement — before going to production.

What would come next

  • Scale testing — does quality hold across hundreds of hotels in different languages?
  • Change detection — the useful version monitors for when a hotel's website changes, not just a one-time scan
  • Feedback loop — corrections from hotels or content teams should inform future scans
  • Liability tier — accessibility and allergy attributes need stricter treatment than "pet-friendly"
