AI Hotel Attribute Scanner

A prototype that crawls hotel websites and uses Claude AI to automatically detect amenities — built to test whether AI could cut weeks of manual content work down to minutes.

The problem

Data showed it was taking weeks, not days, to get new hotels live on the platform. A big part of the bottleneck was attribute completion: hotels fill in their own amenities through an extranet form, and in practice many profiles were incomplete or stale. Content teams were manually checking hotel websites to fill the gaps.

The timing made it worse. We had just added a new layer of attributes to enable more granular search and targeting across our different websites — which made the already-tedious process even more so. Every new attribute was another field hotels had to discover and fill in, and another gap that content teams had to chase down.

The hypothesis: hotel websites already contain most of the attribute information we need. A hotel that has a Finnish sauna will almost certainly mention it somewhere. If an AI can reliably extract that data from a crawl, we could at minimum pre-fill the form for hotels, or flag stale profiles for review.

How it works

The tool takes a hotel URL, crawls the main page and up to 8 relevant subpages (wellness, rooms, dining, FAQ, etc.), strips boilerplate, and sends the extracted text to Claude with a structured list of ~90 attribute types.

Claude returns each attribute as either high confidence (explicitly mentioned, with a direct quote as evidence) or needs review (implied or ambiguous). High-confidence results could be auto-applied; the needs-review pile gets a human look. The confidence split is what makes this workflow-useful rather than just a novelty.

What I learned

Testing on real hotels, homepage-only scans matched around 36% of known attributes. Adding subpages (rooms, wellness, FAQ) pushed that to 58% — FAQs turned out to be especially high-signal, often the only place hotels mention things like parking, pets, or check-in language. The confidence split worked well: high-confidence results held up; the needs-review pile correctly flagged the ambiguous cases.

The AI is much weaker against marketing language. "State-of-the-art wellness facilities" tells you nothing. That's frustratingly common. And "accuracy" doesn't fully capture the real challenge — the ground truth is what the hotel has, not what they say on their site, and those don't always match. High-stakes attributes (accessibility, allergy-friendly) would need tighter calibration or permanent human review before going to production.

What happened next

The prototype landed well internally. Engineering saw the potential immediately, and the Head of Hotel Contracting — who owned the onboarding problem directly — was enthusiastic about what it could mean for time-to-live.

In the end, we made a build-versus-buy decision and went with a third-party data enrichment provider. They can enrich all of our hotel data — not just attributes — at scale and in multiple languages, which a homegrown solution would have taken much longer to match. The prototype did its job: it proved the concept was viable and gave us confidence that AI-assisted attribute extraction would work, which informed how we evaluated vendors. A prototype that gets replaced by a vendor decision isn't a failure — it's a faster path to the right answer.
