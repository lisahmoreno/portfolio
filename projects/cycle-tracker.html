Cycle Tracker

A daily tracking app I built for myself — designed around cycle-phase awareness and nervous system regulation, with no data leaving your device.

React Vite Recharts Privacy-first
Live demo ↗ GitHub ↗

Why I built it

I started reading about cycle-phase nutrition and training a while ago — the idea that energy levels, stress tolerance, and recovery needs aren't constant across the month but follow a predictable hormonal pattern across the four cycle phases. Most tracking apps either ignore this entirely (basic period trackers) or get so elaborate they become a full-time job (symptom logs with dozens of fields and subscription paywalls).

I wanted something specific: a daily diary that asks exactly the questions I care about and nothing else, stores data locally, and shows me patterns over time. After looking at what was available, I decided it was faster to build it than to compromise on a generic tool.

What it tracks

Seven sections, designed to build a picture of how energy, stress, recovery, and physical signals interact across the cycle:

Cycle phase + energy
Current phase (menstruation/follicular/ovulation/luteal), cycle day, overall energy level
Stress across the day
Morning, midday, and evening stress levels separately — plus a free-text main stressor field
Sleep
Sleep/wake time (duration auto-calculated), quality rating, restlessness flag
Movement
Type and duration, plus whether the exercise felt energising or depleting
Nervous system
What contributed to calming down: breathing, nature, alone time, social connection, etc.
Physical signals
Exhaustion, irritability, concentration, PMS symptoms, inner restlessness

The last section is a single free-text reflection: "What did my body show me today?" — kept deliberately short to stay sustainable.

Design decisions worth explaining

Stress tracked at three points in the day, not averaged. A single "stress level today" question loses information. Knowing stress was low in the morning but spiked in the evening is different from knowing it was medium all day. This granularity cost one extra UI section but adds meaningful pattern data.

Movement quality, not just quantity. Tracking "did the exercise feel energising or depleting" is more useful than duration alone — especially in the context of cycle phases, where the same workout can feel fine in one phase and exhausting in another. This is a data point most fitness apps don't collect.

Fully client-side, no account. Health data about menstrual cycles is sensitive. Removing the backend entirely means there's nothing to breach, no terms of service to read, and no subscription to forget to cancel. All data lives in browser localStorage. The PDF export was specifically added so you can share relevant data with a doctor without screenshots.

Building for yourself is a different discipline than building for users. You can skip the user research and skip the edge cases you'll never hit — but you also don't get the forcing function of someone else's confusion. The things I found myself avoiding (the reflection field, filling in the stress sliders) were useful signals about what the daily friction points actually were.
